Week 1 — Lesson 1

What is Accounting?

Accounting is the process of identifying, recording, summarizing, and reporting financial information. It is called "The Language of Business."

🔍

Identifying

Spotting which events involve money — e.g., selling a product, paying rent.

✏️

Recording

Writing down every money-related event in the books systematically.

📊

Summarizing

Adding everything up into totals — daily, monthly, yearly.

📄

Reporting

Presenting the results to owners, banks, investors, and government.

Types of Accounting

🏦

Financial Accounting

Prepares reports for outsiders — investors, banks, regulators. Example: annual company report.

📋

Managerial Accounting

Prepares reports for insiders — managers — to plan and make decisions. Example: monthly budget.

⚙️

Cost Accounting

Tracks the cost of making products or services. Example: cost to produce one software module.

🧾

Tax Accounting

Handles tax calculations and government compliance. Example: filing income tax returns.

Users of Accounting Information

🏢

Internal Users

Managers, employees, directors, owners, department heads — people inside the company who make day-to-day decisions.

🌍

External Users

Investors, banks, suppliers, government, tax authorities — people outside the company who rely on honest reports.

Week 1 — Core Concept

The Accounting Equation

The single most important rule in all of accounting. Every transaction must keep this balanced.

The Foundation of Bookkeeping

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity

Extended: Assets = Liabilities + (Capital + Revenue − Expenses − Drawings)

💰

Assets (What you HAVE)

Cash, equipment, supplies, furniture, accounts receivable. These are resources owned by the business.

📤

Liabilities (What you OWE)

Bank loans, accounts payable, notes payable. These are debts the business must repay.

👤

Equity (What is YOURS)

Owner's Equity = Assets − Liabilities. What remains for the owner after all debts are paid.

Transaction Examples (Week 1)

Each transaction must keep the equation balanced.

1

Owner invests Tk. 1,00,000 cash

Owner puts own money into business → business gets cash, owner's equity increases.

Cash ↑ 1,00,000
Equity ↑ 1,00,000
2

Bought furniture for Tk. 20,000 cash

One asset (cash) converts to another asset (furniture). Total assets unchanged.

Cash ↓ 20,000
Furniture ↑ 20,000
3

Bought computer on credit for Tk. 30,000

Got an asset (computer) but didn't pay yet → now owe the supplier (liability increases).

Computer ↑ 30,000
A/P ↑ 30,000
Week 2 — Lesson 1

Ethics in Financial Reporting

Ethics means doing what is right and honest. In accounting, it means recording money truthfully.

01

Recognize the Situation

Use your personal ethics to identify when something feels wrong. Many organizations provide written codes of ethics for guidance.

02

Identify the Elements

Who are the stakeholders — people who may be harmed or helped? What are the responsibilities of everyone involved?

03

Choose the Best Alternative

Weigh the impact of each option on all stakeholders. Select the most ethical choice considering all consequences.

⚠️ Famous Unethical Accounting Scandals

Enron (USA), Parmalat (Italy), Satyam Computer Services (India), AIG (USA) — all collapsed due to dishonest financial reporting. This is why ethics in accounting is so critical.

Week 2 — GAAP

Accounting Standards (GAAP)

GAAP = Generally Accepted Accounting Principles. Think of it as the rulebook for accounting — everyone must follow the same rules.

Definition

A common set of standards and procedures for financial reporting used by all businesses.

Purpose

Ensures financial data stays relevant, reliable, and comparable across all companies.

Importance

Lets investors and creditors fairly compare different companies' financial health.

Convergence

FASB (USA) and IASB (International) are working to reduce differences between GAAP and IFRS globally.

Measurement Principles

📌 Historical Cost (Cost Principle)

Record assets at the original price paid when purchased, not today's market value. This keeps records objective and verifiable.

Example: Bought laptop for Tk. 80,000 in 2024. Now worth Tk. 45,000. Still recorded as Tk. 80,000.

📌 Fair Value Principle

Record assets and liabilities at their current market value — what you would get if you sold them today.

Example: Shares bought for Tk. 50,000. Current market value Tk. 75,000. Reported as Tk. 75,000.

Fundamental Accounting Assumptions

💱

Monetary Unit Assumption

Only record things that can be expressed in money. You cannot record "we have a great team" — there's no number for that.

🏗️

Economic Entity Assumption

The business and the owner are completely separate. Owner's personal expenses must never enter the business books. Zero owner costs in business books.

Week 2 — Lesson 2

Analyzing Business Transactions

A transaction is any economic event that affects money. Every transaction must affect at least two accounts and keep the equation balanced.

Softbyte SA — All 10 Transactions

1

Shareholders invest $15,000 cash

Owners put money in → business gets cash, share capital increases.

Cash ↑15,000
Share Capital ↑15,000
2

Buy equipment for $7,000 cash

Cash converts to equipment — both are assets, total unchanged.

Cash ↓7,000
Equipment ↑7,000
3

Buy supplies worth $1,600 on credit

Got supplies now, will pay later → liability (A/P) increases.

Supplies ↑1,600
A/P ↑1,600
4

Earned $1,200 cash for services

Did work and got paid → cash up, revenue up (increases equity).

Cash ↑1,200
Revenue ↑1,200
5

Advertising bill of $250 (unpaid)

Used advertising service, haven't paid yet → payable and expense both increase.

A/P ↑250
Expense ↑250
6

Earned $3,500 ($1,500 cash + $2,000 credit)

Did more work — part paid now (cash), part to be collected later (A/R).

Cash ↑1,500 · A/R ↑2,000
Revenue ↑3,500
7

Paid rent & salary $1,500 cash

Operating costs paid → cash down, expenses up (equity decreases).

Cash ↓1,500
Expense ↑1,500
8

Paid the $250 advertising bill

Settling a previous liability → cash down, A/P down.

Cash ↓250
A/P ↓250
9

Collected $600 from credit customer

Customer pays what they owed → cash up, A/R down (no new revenue).

Cash ↑600
A/R ↓600
10

Paid $1,300 dividends to shareholders

Owners take profit out → cash down, equity decreases via dividends.

Cash ↓1,300
Dividends ↑1,300
